My AI Writing Pipeline: How 5 Tools Let Me Publish 3 Posts a Day

By dengf  |  Published: July 15, 2026  |  Category: Behind the Scenes

People ask me this all the time: "You run this site solo — where do all the articles come from?" Honest answer: about half I write myself, and half come off a pipeline. Today I'm laying the whole thing out — my complete AI writing pipeline, no secrets held back.

Let me be clear up front: this is NOT "let AI write everything and hit publish." Do that and you get exactly the kind of "low-value content" that got this blog rejected by AdSense the first time around. The whole thing lives or dies on step three — the human pass.

The 5 Tools, and What Each One Does

1. GitHub Trending — the topic source

Every morning I skim the AI-related repos in Trending and judge which ones are worth writing about. This step can't be automated, because "is this worth writing about?" is a subjective call.

2. DeepSeek — first drafts

Why DeepSeek instead of GPT? It's cheap and it writes well. And my prompt isn't "write an article about X" — it's "you're someone who has actually used X; write down your real experience." That framing decides whether the draft has any human texture to build on.

3. Claude — killing the "AI smell"

DeepSeek's drafts are full of filler phrases — "in conclusion," "without a doubt," that sort of thing. I have Claude swap all the formulaic phrasing for something conversational, then I go through it once more myself. This step eats the most time, but it's the most valuable.

4. Unsplash + Remove.bg — images

Unsplash for free photos, Remove.bg to strip out watermarks or backgrounds. Faster than shooting my own, cheaper than a paid stock library.

5. My own scripts — publishing

A script generates a ~150-character SEO description, then posts directly through the Blogger API. Publishing is fully automated — but the content stage is very much hands-on.

Mistakes I've Paid For

Mistake 1: Pure AI output got me rejected. Early on I got lazy and let the script publish fully automatically. The articles read like stitched-together spec sheets. That's exactly why AdSense rejected me the first time. Things only turned around once I added the human rewrite step.

Mistake 2: Repetitive topics. For a stretch I published five "Best AI X Tools" posts in a row — even I found them watery. Now I enforce a rule: at least one in every three posts has to be a hands-on test or an opinion piece.

Mistake 3: Image licensing. I once used a "free" image from some stock site and got a complaint email. Since then it's Unsplash (permissive license) or my own screenshots, nothing else.

Why I Don't Just Go Fully Automatic

I tried. Fully automated, I could push 20 posts a day — but a site like that doesn't survive three months. Google flags you as a content farm, and readers vote with their feet. I'd rather publish 3 living posts a day than 20 dead ones.

FAQ

Q: Do you really edit every single post yourself?
A: The first draft is 90% AI, but the final published version has been through me at least once, with every "AI smell" phrase scrubbed out. If my name (dengf) is on it, I stand behind the content.

Q: What does it cost?
A: DeepSeek + Claude runs under $20 a month, and images are free. That's two orders of magnitude cheaper than hiring a writer.

Q: Can a beginner copy this?
A: Yes. Start with "pick topics yourself + AI writes the draft + you edit it." Don't jump straight to full automation.

Q: Won't readers notice it's AI-written?
A: If you edit it well, readers just think "this person writes fast and clean" — they don't care whether AI was involved. The tool is a tool; the judgment is yours.

Final Thoughts

The point of a pipeline isn't "being lazy" — it's "hand the repetitive labor to machines, keep the judgment for yourself." The reason this blog is still alive isn't that I publish a lot; it's that every post passed through my hands at least once.
